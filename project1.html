Kaleidosaur.



Role :

Creative Lead. UI/UX. Product Design.

started :

Sept 2018.

completed :

May 2019.

Kaleidosaur was a web based, open-source, abstract art generator that combined cutting edge machine learning and generative design. Through countless hours of research and development, my capstone team (Michael Rascati, Kevin Altschuler) worked to bring this project to life. I took on the role of creative director for this project, responsible not only for the visual design, but the infrastructure and direction of the application.



The goal of our project was to create a web application which allowed users to explore a computer’s capacity to create abstract visual representations of real-world objects. By utilizing this format, we had the freedom to educate and inform others about what was possible with the technology and to further inspire exploration into the field. From a user standpoint, building an intuitive interface and compelling project ensured that users would be able to enjoy the creative output as well as the process of generating abstract art.





Building off the inspirational works of Chris Cummins' Generative Art and Tom White's "Perception Engine,” Kaleidosaur worked by taking an input tag and slowly recreating the internal representation of this tag based on a massive image library (Imagenet).



Take the tag "dog" for instance. Referencing Imagenet for the label "dog,” Kaleidosaur was able to generate a random seed of polygons tailored to the user input. Parameters like population size, opacity, number of polygons, number of vertices, etc. were user controlled. Using a genetic algorithm, Kaleidosaur continued to mutate and essentially "breed" new seeds of polygons from the fittest of each generation by checking the image against the references and slowly moving toward the end goal of "dog.”



In essence, we were creating an abstract image, the ideal, of an entity through the lens of a computer. As a result, many interesting questions were raised. What characteristics determined a "dog" through the lens of a computer? Was it the four legs? The fur? Or, was it something tangential found in many dog photos, like grass? The qualities that humans viewed as essential for creating a mental model might not be aligned with a computer’s model at all.









Though the project went through several iterations of design, our core philosophy of what the project should be remained constant. Our guiding principle was to generate a simple and intuitive interface that allowed users to easily navigate the web app with all its functionality, along with a visual design that reflected the type of abstract art we were trying to create. The front end of the project was built upon a solid foundation of research, ethnography, user testing, and competitive analysis.


We started with a series of wireframes that went through the various workflows. A one page layout that allowed the user to immediately jump in to the abstract art generator was utilized. This layout also allowed the user to learn about the project while the generator was running, a feature that was important from a technical perspective as we were unsure of how long the process would take. Users were able to start creating their art and while the generator was working, they could read about our research and explore art in the gallery.



Next, we began working on low-mid fidelity mockups. After several iterations (when we had a better idea of what our generated artwork would look like), we began playing with a visual philosophy that can best be described as “brutalist.” We wanted to create something that was unconventional, defied traditional design principles, reflected the exploratory and abstract nature of our art, yet remained simple and easy to navigate for the user. Making use of bold, heavy, extended fonts, bright colors, and large imposing shapes, we began shaping the front-end. It was imperative that the design was kinetic.



Though our project wasn’t designed specifically for mobile use, we adapted the site to be workable on these devices. The mobile design had the benefit of several iterations worth of web content to work from, and the mockup process was fairly simple. The mobile mockups went through two phases of replication and testing, with the majority of changes revolving around the primary workflow.



The final high fidelity mockups made use of the design principles that we tested, with a fair amount of refinement. We used interactive backgrounds, scrolling text animations, adaptive color themes, and a unique visual design. These animations were all interactive in some way, though refined to a point where they were not obtrusive to the user. The size of the site afforded us limited navigational functionality, though users were still able to proceed to each of the sections with ease. Though working in such a tricky design space proved challenging, it was a fulfilling process, and the final project spoke for itself.




Unfortunately due to limited funding and time, Kaleidosaur had to be disconnected from the Google Cloud Computing services and is no longer functional. However, the work can be found on GitHub here. Given the time and resources, I would love to return to this project one day to fully flesh out the rendering module and all of its capabilities!
